1 Introduction

Trans-Portal prepared this Master File for XYZ Corporation ("XYZ" or the “Company”) for the year ended November 2015 (“2015”). This Master File accords with Chapter V of the Transfer Pricing Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises (“MNE”) and Tax A dmi nistrations issued by the Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development (“OECD Guidelines”), published in November 2015. This Master File complements the Company’s local country files (“Local Files”) prepared to address the specific transfer pricing requirements of the local country. Together, the Master File and Local Files comprise the Company’s 2015 Transfer Pricing Documentation (“Documentation”). The Master File contains information relevant for its worldwide operations, including Canada, China, Germany, Denmark, Switzerland , the United Kingdom (“U.K.”), and the United States (“U.S.”).

1.1 Purpose

This document was prepared in accordance with the recommendations of the OECD transfer pricing documentation concept (“master file and local file approach”), which is based on the OECD Guidelines. The Guidelines were revised in 2015 as a result of the outcome of the Base Erosion and Profit Shifting (“BEPS”) Project, focused on the alignment of transfer pricing outcomes with value creation. According to Paragraph 18 of the OECD Transfer Pricing Documentation and Country-by-Country Reporting Action 13, the Master File “should provide an overview of the MNE Group Business, including the nature of its global business operations, its transfer pricing policies, and its global allocation of income and economic activity in order to assist tax a dmi nistrations in evaluating the presence of significant transfer pricing risk. ”

The Master File is a general document that provides an overview of the worldwide operations of the Company and its subsidiaries. This includes the transfer pricing policy, the nature of its global business operations and its global economic activity and income allocation for 2018. A Master File provides an overview to place the Company’s transfer pricing practices in its global financial, legal, and economic context.

According to the OECD Guidelines, transactions between controlled parties must be priced on an “arm’s length” basis, or as if the related enterprises were dealing with each other as independent or unrelated parties. Under the OECD Guidelines, the authoritative statement of the arm’s length principle is provided in paragraph 1 of Article 9 of the OECD Model Tax Convention, which forms the basis of bilateral tax treaties involving OECD member countries and an increasing number of non-member countries.

Article 9 provides:

[Where] conditions are made or imposed between the two [associated] enterprises in their commercial or financial relations which differ from those which would be made between independent enterprises, then any profits which would, but for those conditions, have accrued to one of the enterprises, but, by reason of those conditions, have not so accrued, may be included in the profits of that enterprise and taxed accordingly.

3 Description of the Company’s Business

This section outlines the Company’s products, major markets, and competition. The Company’s enterprise-specific value-added activities, as well as certain sources of competitive advantages and business strategies are described.

3.1 Business Segments and Product Overview

XYZ Corporation was founded in 1967 and is headquartered in Peabody, Massachusetts. It designs, manufactures, and sells security systems and subsystems, medical imaging systems, and ultrasound systems to original equipment manufacturers (“OEMs”) and end users primarily for the medical and airport security markets worldwide. It operates in three business segments: Security and Detection, Imaging, and Imaging.

3.1.1 Security and Detection

The Security and Detection segment designs and manufactures automated threat detection systems for aviation baggage inspection applications. It offers checked baggage, checkpoint computed tomography (“CT”), and high speed in-line baggage handling systems; as well as rapid DNA analysis systems to analyze multiple human DNA samples. This segment sells its products primarily through multinational partners. The Security and Detection segment accounted for approximately 28 percent of the Company’s total net revenue in 2018. However, since this segment does not engage in intercompany transactions, it is not a focus in the Company’s Documentation.

3.1.2 Imaging

The Imaging segment is comprised of many different products. Among the CT products offered are CT detector systems, data acquisition systems, data management systems, and integrated CT systems. The magnetic resonance imaging (“MRI”) products include gradient and radio frequency amplifiers. Additionally, the Imaging segment offers motion control devices for use in computer-controlled automation systems primarily for the semiconductor, food and beverage, and laboratory automation markets. This segment sells its products through multinational OEMs. The Imaging segment accounted for approximately 38 percent of the Company’s total net revenue in 2018.

3.1.2.1 CT Products

XYZ is an industry leader in the development and sale of CT detector systems, data acquisition systems (“DAS”), data management systems (“DMS”), and fully integrated CT systems that drive the image processing in OEM CT imaging scanners sold around the world.

 Detector Systems – Convert the x-ray energy in CT machines to analog signals for further processing in the DAS.

 DAS – Process the signals created by the detectors and feed them as a digital stream to a reconstruction computer to create high resolution images.

 DMS – Consists of both the detector array and the DAS in one package. Provides OEM customers better integration that allows for lower costs and faster time to market.

 Fully Integrated CT Systems – Mounts the components of the CT system (detectors, DAS, x-ray tube, and power supplies) on a rotating ring assembly called a gantry. The ring enables the components of the CT systems to rotate around the patient at speeds up to 300 revolutions per minute for high resolution imaging.

3.1.2.2 Magnetic Resonance Imaging

The Company supplies two key enabling subsystems for OEM producers of MRI equipment: gradient amplifiers and radio frequency (“RF”) amplifiers. XYZ has developed a range of amplifier solutions ranging from low-magnetic-field systems to ultra-high-magnetic-field systems. The ability to provide high power levels with fast response, low noise, and industry-leading reliability enables its OEM customers to deliver innovative technologies such as larger opening MRI scanners, which address growing needs related to patient obesity and comfort.

 Gradient Amplifiers – High power systems used to drive a set of coils located inside the MRI system and around the patient. The coils energize the atomic structure of a patient’s anatomy in order to create tissue structure images. Gradient amplifiers must be designed at specific power levels for different MRI field strengths.

 RF Amplifiers – Power systems used to control another set of coils within the MRI system that are used to read back the signals from the anatomy generated by the gradient coils. The signals are then processed in a reconstruction computer to create images. RF amplifiers must have a very high signal-to-noise ratio to produce the cleanest images.

3.1.3 Imaging

The Imaging segment designs and manufactures medical Imaging systems under the XYZ Imaging brand for use in general imaging applications. The Imaging segment accounted for approximately 34 percent of the Company’s total net revenue in 2018.

Imaging products are offered through the XYZ Subs and through a network of independent distributors in Eastern Europe, the Middle East, Asia, and internationally. The business was founded in 1972 in Herley, China. It has additional offices in Copenhagen, China and Peabody, Massachusetts.

The product portfolio under the XYZ Imaging trade name consists primarily of two main product families: XYZ and Anonymous systems, which are used primarily for guiding procedures in imaging.

The Imaging segment also designs and manufactures advanced Imaging probes and transducers sold to OEM customers. Using its advanced acoustic design and manufacturing capability, the Imaging segment provides a variety of transducers to OEMs.

3.2 Profit Drivers

Though products and customers for each business segment differ, Company management views its major profit drivers across all segments in order of importance are:

 Technology – historical and continual development;

 Customer relationships; and

 Centralization and cost management.

3.2.1 Technology

XYZ is a technology company at its core. Each segment operates in the premium end of its respective market, with niche expertise in its own product lines. Innovation and anticipation of customer needs is vital to the long-term success of the Company. In the Imaging segment, XYZ’s research and development (“R&D”) team must make it more advantageous for the customer to outsource development of products to XYZ rather than use the customer’s own internal R&D team. In the Imaging segment, the Company strives to continue innovation in its niche as a premium provider of surgical ultrasound products.

3.2.2 Customer Relationships

The Company’s ten largest customers accounted for more than half of net revenue in 2018. These customers include Koninklijke Philips Electronics N.V., Siemens AG, and Toshiba Corporation in the Imaging segment. Loss of any individual customer would have a significant impact on income. Recently, for example, Toshiba Corporation insourced its R&D for CT products, which reduced revenue for that product line by approximately $40 million. The Company incorporates customer suggestions and feedback and strives to stay ahead of customer demands to ensure next generation products are released timely.

The Imaging segment mainly serves surgeons and the institutions in which they work (hospitals, doctors’ offices, and government facilities). The Company estimates its personnel have trained more than half of the physicians who use inter-operative ultrasound globally. In focusing on the surgeon and ensuring the products are fully sterilizable, the Company differentiates itself from others in the market.

3.2.3 Centralization and Cost Management

As in many industries, cost reduction and cost management are an ongoing profit driver. The management team at XYZ-US is increasingly focused on cost reductions to ensure competitiveness with the Company’s R&D services to OEM customers. The team is increasing production efficiencies, reducing logistics costs, and centralizing certain regional activities and functions for each individual segment. The Company anticipates this focus will continue over the foreseeable future as part of its longer-term strategy for growth and enhanced profitability.

3.3 Competition

The Company operates in highly competitive industries and is subject to competition based on product design, performance, pricing, quality, and service offerings. Management believes its innovative engineering and product reliability have been important factors in historical growth. In many instances, the Company’s products conform to precise specifications and may carry a higher price than analogous products manufactured by the competition. The trend of consolidation in the medical device industry and among the Company’s customers could result in greater competition and pricing pressures.

The Company’s competitors include divisions of larger, more diversified organizations, as well as specialized companies, some of whom have greater resources and larger staff than the Company. Many of the Company’s OEM customers have the ability to internally design and manufacture the products the Company manufactures for them. The Company faces competition from the R&D groups and manufacturing operations of existing and potential customers, who continually compare the costs and benefits of internal research, product development, and manufacturing against the costs and benefits of outsourcing.

3.3.1 Imaging Segment

Competition in the Imaging segment is based on product design, performance, pricing, quality, and service. The Company believes its innovative engineering and product reliability have been important factors in its historical growth. Systems and subsystems are customized for the needs of XYZ’s customers. In many cases, due to the limited number of companies with comparable technology, selection of XYZ by its OEM customers for the design and manufacture of Imaging products is more a result of the “make-or-buy” decision of the individual OEM customers than competition from others in the field.

4 Functional Analysis

A functional analysis is a method of finding and organizing facts about a business in terms of its functions, risks, and assets to identify how these characteristics are divided between the controlled parties involved in the Company’s intercompany transactions. The functional analysis provides the factual foundation for selecting and applying the transfer pricing method. The Master File provides a Company-wide overview of the functional analysis. The functional analysis is consistent with the business’s key value drivers and the MNE’s supply chain.

4.1 Functions

Key functions relevant to the Company’s operations are described in the following paragraphs.

4.1.1 Strategic Management

Worldwide strategic management for all segments is performed by XYZ-US management personnel. XYZ-EOS provides input regarding Imaging strategy, but strategic decisions for Imaging ultimately lie with XYZ-US management.

4.1.2 Sourcing and Procurement

Most Group products are composed of internally designed electronic and mechanical elements, including proprietary integrated circuits, printed circuit boards, detectors, power supplies, and displays. When components are manufactured by third party suppliers, this occurs in accordance with XYZ-US or XYZ-EOS specifications. For customer-specific orders, the Company orders raw materials and components from sole-source suppliers. If a given component ceases to be available, it might become necessary to modify a product design to adapt to a substitute component, or to purchase new tooling to enable a new supplier to manufacture the component, either of which could result in additional expense and/or delay in product sales. XYZ-US and XLLC carry a safety stock of raw materials and components to ensure timely delivery to customers.

4.1.3 Manufacturing

In-house manufacturing of Company products is performed at XYZ-US and XLLC. XYZ-US is responsible for production scheduling, planning, and specifications for itself and XLLC. XLLC takes direction from XYZ-US in terms of what it produces. XLLC manufactures Imaging products based on XYZ-US product designs for sale into China as well as for sale back to XYZ-US. When XLLC sells Imaging products directly into China, XLLC may perform minor, routine customization to adapt products to suit local market needs, though any customization is based on XYZ-US prototypes.

XLLC manufactures Imaging products based on XYZ-EOS product designs, and, until they are phased out, XYZ-C product designs. There is no additional customization for Imaging products performed at XLLC. XYZ-US also manufactures finished goods for the Imaging segment using product designs historically developed by XYZ-EOS.

4.1.4 Service and Repairs

After-sales service is an important activity for both Imaging and Imaging. In Imaging, repairs are performed at XYZ-US and, to a lesser extent, at XLLC. XLLC only repairs products it manufactures on-site. In Imaging, the XYZ Subs have field personnel responsible for minor repairs. XYZ-EOS and XYZ-US perform both repairs in the field and in-house if the product cannot be repaired at the customer’s site.

The Imaging segment also sells service contracts related to the Imaging systems. Service contracts include varying price-based levels of included services. Repairs and maintenance activities covered by these service contracts are performed by the Group selling entity.

4.1.5 Marketing and Sales

Marketing and sales activities for the Imaging segment are performed by XYZ-US with some activities performed locally at XLLC. Sales personnel for the Imaging segment at XLLC report directly to the head of global sales and marketing at XYZ- US.

XYZ-US is responsible for the Imaging segment’s marketing strategy, participation in exhibitions, and marketing copy. Marketing costs are borne by XYZ-EOS as part of the transfer pricing calculation in the sale of goods. Sales in the Imaging segment are carried out by the XYZ Subs in their respective countries, XYZ-US in the United States, XYZ-Can in Canada, XYZ-EOS in China, and third-party distributors in other locations. The XYZ Subs are responsible for tracking and managing the reputation of the Group in their markets. There are approximately 78 sales personnel at XYZ-US and approximately 20 personnel on the payroll in XLLC for XYZ sales.

4.1.6 Price Setting

XYZ-US is responsible for pricing products sold in the Imaging segment. When XLLC sells to local customers in China, it may negotiate pricing, but must receive approval from XYZ-US on any final price decisions. XYZ-US establishes price lists for Imaging product sales with input from XYZ-EOS, though the XYZ Subs sell most of their products below list price. It is standard in the industry to discount pricing from the listed price.

4.1.7 Legal and Regulatory

Legal and regulatory matters concerning the Imaging group are outsourced to a third-party legal expert. An in-house attorney employed by XYZ-US oversees the Imaging segment’s legal matters and assist with Imaging specific matters from time to time. Regulations vary by country, so the XYZ Subs each manage their own registrations. The legal department ensures external contracts are in order.

4.1.8 Research and Development

R&D is a significant value driver in the Company’s business. The Company maintains a constant and comprehensive R&D program directed toward the creation of new products, the improvement and refinement of present products, and the expansion of applications. Certain R&D projects are funded by customers, typically OEM customers. Such funding is generally treated as engineering revenue, with the associated costs classified as engineering cost of sales.

The Company evaluates developing technologies in areas where they have technological or marketing expertise for possible investment or acquisition. It intends to continue to invest in R&D and focus internal and external investments in fields that offer the greatest potential for near and long-term growth. The Company invests in products that have a demonstrable clinical impact and value to the healthcare system and through which the Company can benefit from its core competencies and global infrastructure. The Company invests approximately USD 70 million per year on R&D on a consolidated, worldwide basis.

4.2 Risks

Business risks are those circumstances that may expose a company to the possibility of loss or damage. We discuss how the relevant risks are borne by entities in the Group in their normal course of business below.

4.2.1 Market Risk

Market risk arises when a company is subject to adverse sales conditions due to increased competition, declining conditions within the market, or the inability to develop markets or position products to reach targeted customers.

Market risk for Imaging is borne by XYZ-US. XLLC bears a limited amount of market risk in China. In Imaging, the XYZ Subs bear some market risk as they are fully reliant on local customer purchases. Overall market risk lies with XYZ-US and XYZ-EOS as the headquarters and entrepreneur entities in the Group.

4.2.2 Inventory Risk

Inventory risk relates to the potential for losses associated with carrying inventory. Losses include obsolescence, shrinkage, or market collapse such that products are only saleable at prices that are inadequate to cover a company’s product costs.

XYZ-US bears inventory risk for raw materials and work-in-process for itself and for XLLC with respect to production for both Imaging and Imaging products. Finished goods are generally not kept on hand. Some components and parts are kept on hand by XYZ-US and XLLC in case they are needed for production, specific warranty services, or other product repairs or to fulfill service contracts.

4.2.3 Product Quality / Warranty Risk

Product quality or warranty risks arise when a company’s products fail to perform at accepted or advertised standards. Product liability risk is borne by a company when its products fail to perform at contractual standards and the company is required to correct defective product performance.

In Imaging, XYZ-US bears product quality and warranty risks for all products it produces. For products produced by XLLC, XYZ-US bears the same risk as it covers any costs associated with repair if needed.

In Imaging, product / warranty risk within the initial warranty period is borne by XYZ-US. In the event there is a significant event related to product liability, this would be borne by all Group entities in the Imaging segment to the extent that it damages the Company’s brand and reputation. For extended warranties and service, XYZ-EOS is responsible for the costs associated with repair in most of Asia and all of Europe while XYZ-US is responsible for these costs in North America.

4.2.4 Credit Risk

When a company supplies products or services to a customer in advance of payment, the company bears failure-to-pay, credit, and collections risk.

As the seller of record for all Imaging products, XYZ-US bears credit risk. In Imaging, credit risk is borne by each selling entity, though credit risk is low based on conservative credit terms offered by the Group.

4.2.5 Foreign Exchange Risk

A company is exposed to foreign exchange risk if its profits are affected, beyond its control, by fluctuations in currency rates. While a company can often manage short-term foreign exchange risk for many currencies by hedging, it will be unable to eliminate its risk if the majority of its customers are located outside of its country.

XYZ-US is ultimately responsible for foreign exchange risk in its intercompany transactions with XLLC. XYZ-US bears foreign exchange risk as it sells to the XYZ Subs in their local currencies and the XYZ Subs sell in their local currencies. Since XYZ-EOS purchases from XYZ-US in USD and sells in Danish Kroner, EURO, and USD, it bears foreign exchange risk as well. XYZ-EOS generally buys a similar amount in USD that it sells, so it enjoys a natural hedge.

4.3 Assets

Assets are typically an important part of a company’s ability to compete effectively and deliver value to customers. Companies own and employ two types of assets – tangible assets and intangible assets. Tangible assets can be seen and touched; they include items that can be reproduced, such as inventory, buildings, capital equipment, and land. Intangible assets cannot be seen or touched; they include technology, know-how, brand, and relationships. Intangible assets are typically a key factor considered in characterizing entities involved in controlled transactions and selecting transfer pricing methods.

4.3.1 Tangible Assets

Each XYZ entity owns furniture and fixtures, computer hardware, and office equipment. All of these assets are routine for the course of business. 5 Group Intangible Assets

Intangible assets are assets that are not physical or financial, such as patents, know-how, and trade names. These can be owned or controlled for use in commercial activities. If use of an intangible asset occurred in a controlled transaction between non-related parties in comparable circumstances, such usage would be compensated. In transfer pricing cases where intangibles are involved, the economic ownership of intangibles is determined by identifying which entity is responsible for the functions regarding the development, enhancement, maintenance, protection, and exploitation (“DEMPE” Functions) associated with the intangible asset. This section provides an overview of the Company’s intangibles that are important for transfer pricing purposes, as well as which entities legally and/or economically own them.
